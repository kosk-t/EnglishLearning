News Summaries

Text 1 — Politics

Parliament vote and coalition arithmetic

Japan’s Lower House will vote on a new prime minister on Tuesday, October 21. Liberal Democratic Party (LDP) leader Sanae Takaichi regained momentum after a turbulent week by advancing talks with the right‑leaning Japan Innovation Party (Ishin), following the LDP’s split with long‑time partner Komeito. The LDP holds 196 of 465 seats—short of the 233 needed for a simple majority. If Ishin backs Takaichi, the combined tally would be close, and the LDP may still seek support from smaller groups for the final votes. Opposition parties have floated a joint candidate, but policy gaps make coordination difficult. Even if Takaichi wins, a fragmented legislature could slow her economic agenda, forcing careful bargaining on spending, deregulation, and other priorities.

Text 2 — Economy

Guarding against exchange‑rate “volatility”

Japan’s finance minister Katsunobu Kato told global counterparts that authorities must monitor risks and guard against excessive foreign‑exchange swings that could harm economic and financial stability. In a statement to an IMF committee, he cited an uncertain outlook shaped by trade tensions and geopolitics, along with long‑term shifts in artificial intelligence, climate change, and demographics. While announcing no specific action, Kato stressed the need to prevent disorderly currency moves. The message suggests Japan wants to keep policy options open—potentially including coordination with partners—so that big, sudden moves in the yen do not unsettle companies or households. Overall, the emphasis was on stability rather than dramatic market shocks.

Text 3 — Society

Planned survey on online hate speech

The Justice Ministry is considering Japan’s first nationwide survey of online hate speech aimed at foreign visitors and residents, with work planned for fiscal 2026. The study would examine posts on X and other major platforms and collect consultation records from local governments to gauge the scale and impact of abuse. About ¥70 million has been requested for the budget. Under a 2016 law, hate speech refers to calls for blanket exclusion of specific groups without reasonable cause. While street demonstrations have declined, officials say anonymous online attacks have increased, making the overall picture harder to see. The survey is intended to inform future countermeasures and guide cooperation with platforms and municipalities.

D) Speaking Prompt (30 seconds)

Topic: Should Japan conduct a nationwide online hate‑speech survey? In 30 seconds, state your position and mention one benefit and one concern.
  • Briefly restate the issue in your own words.
  • Give your stance (for or against) with one reason.
  • Add one concern or trade‑off to acknowledge the other side.
  • Finish with a practical suggestion (e.g., how to protect privacy).

Answers & Explanations

A) Reading

  1. Q1: B — The LDP seeks external backing before the vote, and a fragmented parliament could hinder policy. Evidence: Text 1 notes talks with Ishin and the risk of a “fragmented legislature.”
  2. Q2: B — Japan Innovation Party (Ishin). Evidence: Text 1 states the LDP is negotiating with Ishin after splitting with Komeito.
  3. Q3: B — Coordination or action is possible if moves are “disorderly.” Evidence: Text 2 stresses guarding against excessive volatility rather than fixing a rate or hiking now.
  4. Q4: B — “Volatility” means big, hard‑to‑predict swings in price. Evidence: Text 2 links it to exchange‑rate movements that unsettle the economy.
  5. Q5: False — The survey is being considered for fiscal 2026; it is not completed and not slated for FY2025 release. Evidence: Text 3 says “considering” and “planned for fiscal 2026.”

B) Vocabulary & Collocation

  1. V1: A — coalition: an alliance of parties or groups that cooperate to govern. Example: “The two parties formed a coalition after the election.”
  2. V2: B — volatility: rapid, unpredictable change, especially in prices. Example: “High market volatility makes budgeting hard for importers.”
  3. V3: B — survey: a structured study for collecting data from many sources or people. Example: “The ministry will run a nationwide survey on online abuse.”
  4. V4: “form/forge a coalition.” Use: “The LDP hopes to form a coalition with Ishin before Tuesday’s vote.”
  5. V5: “conduct/carry out/run a (the) survey.” Use: “Officials plan to conduct a survey of social‑media posts.”

C) Grammar Cloze

  1. G1: who — Use who for people as the subject in a relative clause (“a prime minister who must…”).
  2. G2: against — The fixed phrase is “be vigilant against (a threat).” Preposition shows what you guard against.

D) Speaking — Model Outline

Hook → A sharp rise in toxic posts makes policy choices urgent. Point → Support the survey to map the problem clearly. Evidence → Anonymous abuse is under‑reported; nationwide data guides targeted remedies. Counter → Privacy and free‑speech risks exist; avoid over‑collection and vague definitions. Close → Proceed with transparent methods, independent oversight, and sunset rules.
