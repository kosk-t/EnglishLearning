Problems

A) Reading Comprehension (5 questions)

Read the news summaries (100–150 words each), then answer the questions. Types: Main idea / Detail / Inference / Vocab-in-Context / True–False.

Passage 1 — OpenAI backs an AI-made feature film

OpenAI is helping produce Critterz, an animated feature designed to showcase what generative AI can do in filmmaking. The project—run with partners Native Foreign and Vertigo Films—aims for a Cannes 2026 premiere and a worldwide release later that year. According to reports, much of the imagery will be created with OpenAI’s latest tools, while human actors and artists still shape performances and sound. The team plans an accelerated schedule of about nine months and a budget below $30 million, far leaner than typical animated features. Supporters say the experiment could lower barriers for creators and speed up production. Skeptics worry about labor impacts, legal risks, and whether AI-led work can match the originality audiences expect.

Passage 2 — Google publishes exact usage caps for Gemini

Google has finally spelled out how much you can use Gemini under each plan. On the free tier, users get up to five prompts per day with Gemini 2.5 Pro and can generate or edit up to 100 images a day. The company also clarified limits for its research tool: the free tier allows five Deep Research reports per month (using 2.5 Flash), while paid plans raise the ceiling—Pro: 100 prompts/day, Ultra: 500 prompts/day, and up to 1,000 images/day. Deep Research expands to roughly 20 reports/day on Pro and 200/day on Ultra. Google says exact capacity can vary with prompt length and other factors, but the new guidance removes the guesswork.

Passage 3 — UK publisher restructures amid AI and reader shifts

Reach, the company behind the Daily Mirror, Daily Express, and Daily Star, announced a major shake‑up as it adapts to changing reader habits and the growing impact of AI on how news is made and found. Up to 600 roles are at risk, including 321 editorial positions, even as the firm plans to create 135 new jobs and focus more on video, audio, and a live‑news network. Executives call it the biggest reorganization in years; the journalists’ union warns of falling morale. The strategy reflects a broader industry dilemma: publishers must cut costs and retool for digital discovery while trying to protect quality reporting and keep audiences engaged.

C) Grammar Cloze (2 questions)

D) Speaking Prompt (1 task · 30 seconds)

Topic: AI‑made feature films are moving toward mainstream release. In 30 seconds, state your position: Should AI‑driven productions be embraced by major studios? Give one pro and one con from the news context before your conclusion.

Answers & Explanations

A) Reading Comprehension

  1. RC1: To demonstrate that AI can make feature films faster and cheaper. (Passage 1 highlights a low budget and nine‑month schedule to showcase AI’s potential.)
  2. RC2: About nine months; under $30 million. (Those figures are stated as the project’s target timeline and budget.)
  3. RC3: True. (Free tier = 5 prompts/day on Gemini 2.5 Pro.)
  4. RC4: Pressure to adapt to AI’s impact and new reader behavior. (The passage links the restructure to AI and audience shifts.)
  5. RC5: sped‑up. (“Accelerated” contrasts with typical, slower animation schedules.)

B) Vocabulary & Collocation

  1. V1: a fixed limit or maximum amount. Definition: a hard ceiling on quantity or cost. Example: There’s a cap on free image generations per day.
  2. V2: doubt about whether something is true or good. Definition: a feeling of not being convinced. Example: Studios show skepticism about fully AI‑generated movies.
  3. V3: change how an organization is arranged. Definition: reorganize structure, roles, or processes. Example: The publisher will restructure to focus on live news.
  4. V4: usage limits. Use: a standard collocation meaning official caps on quantity. Example: Google posted new usage limits for Gemini.
  5. V5: job cuts. Use: common phrase for reducing staff. Example: The slowdown forced job cuts across the sector.

C) Grammar Cloze

  1. G1: with. Rule: Use with to add accompanying details (timeline/cost) to a main clause.
  2. G2: which. Rule: Non‑restrictive relative clause after a comma takes which (not that).

D) Speaking Prompt — Model Outline (not a full script)

Hook: A big‑studio film made on a start‑up timeline and budget grabs attention.
Point: Embrace AI‑assisted filmmaking when it adds speed and lowers costs.
Evidence: Nine‑month schedule and sub‑$30M target suggest efficiency.
Counter: Safeguard jobs/credit and handle IP risks; newsrooms facing AI pressure show the risks.
Close: Use AI as a tool under human oversight, not a replacement.
